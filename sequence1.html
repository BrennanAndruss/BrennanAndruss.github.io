Sequence I

Literacy Narrative

Reflection

Every project begins with an idea. Before I make a single thing in my game or my level, I think about all the various things I could create and list them all out. Even some of the most ridiculous ideas get added to the list. Once you obtain a full collection of ideas, you can then narrow down the list to your favorite ideas and find the idea that will become the foundation of your game. Writing a paper is no different, especially with a paper as open and expressive as this one. Before writing a single word, you should go through some process of brainstorming, be it through answering prompts or simply writing freely. Only then can you go in and decide what you want to get started writing about. However, choosing something doesn’t mean that list all of a sudden goes away. The writing process is not a straight line, but rather a circle; a cycle of drafting, reviewing, and revising. The process of brainstorming will generate many good ideas, and although your project will center around one or two of those, revisiting and incorporating those other ideas will round out your project and can be the thing that ties it all together.

Drafting

This artifact demonstrates my skill in being able to effectively brainstorm and get ideas down on paper. Just like with a personal journal, I write down whatever ideas come to mind without judgment, enabling me to rapidly generate ideas. For this activity, once all those questions were answered and all the thoughts generated by the prompts were written down, that's when I was able to filter through for the best ideas and biggest takeaways from the exercise, underlining what I liked the most and finding some gems to serve as the foundation of my literacy narrative.

Peer Review

Sometimes, bringing new eyes onto your work can bring new ideas with it. Often a first draft will focus strongly on prominent ideas and certain aspects of the essay, which while good, can result in other ideas and other aspects being overlooked. For this first draft, I got into my journaling process and story much sooner and put a large emphasis on using vivid details and imagery, but I overlooked some of the background information leading into the story. Some of the ideas mentioned in the feedback were things I had thought of and written down in process artifacts, but I didn’t find these details to be as interesting or as important to my story at first. However, getting feedback from peers to elaborate on those background details made me aware of their importance in telling a complete story from beginning to end.

Revising

In revising my narrative, I wanted to strike a balance between addressing the weak points suggested from peer reviews as well as further refine the strengths. This artifact shows the development and clarification of ideas, elaborating on the background information and giving more insight into my perspective in those sections as well as building upon the vivid descriptions and running metaphors present throughout my narrative. The revisions you make to an essay can vary greatly too, from rewriting whole sections to just a single word. For as many sentences and sections I rewrote or expanded, I made just as many micro adjustments to words and short phrases to smooth out some clunky sentences and improve the overall flow of the narrative.
